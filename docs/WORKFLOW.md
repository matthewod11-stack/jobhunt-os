# Where this came from

I'm Matt. This workspace is the cleaned-up version of a system I built while running my own job search, which started in April 2026 and ended in early July when I signed an offer at an AI product company. I work in People/HR, mostly at technical, founder-led companies. Over roughly three months I ran eighteen processes end to end: a hardware startup, an AI-music company, a cloud-infrastructure startup, a frontier-AI lab, a legal-tech company, several early-stage AI startups. Most of them ended in rejection. The system exists because of what those rejections taught me.

## It got built backwards

I didn't sit down and design a toolkit. The first pieces were the obvious ones: tailor a resume to a posting, build a prep doc before a call, write down what happened after. That's the ancestry of /apply, /prep, and /debrief, and for the first month that was the whole system.

The sourcing side came later, out of necessity. Job boards produced almost nothing for my profile: by the time a senior People role is posted, it typically has a recruiter, a pipeline, and hundreds of applicants. The processes that actually went somewhere came from funding news and investor portfolios, companies I found before or outside a posting. That reasoning became /scout and the sourcing playbook in this folder.

/recap came last, when processes started closing and I realized the ending of a search arc held lessons that no single debrief captured. Rejections carry more usable information than advancements do, but only if you extract it before you archive the folder.

## What compounding actually felt like

The honest answer: for the first month, not much. Debriefs felt like journaling. The payoff showed up around month three, when prep docs started citing things I'd learned in other companies' processes. A question that wrecked me in one interview had a worked answer waiting the next time it appeared. The prep doc for my fifth onsite was better than the one for my first, not because I got better at writing prep docs but because the corpus underneath it had gotten denser.

The single most durable artifact is the answer spine: principle, what I've done, what I'd do now, stop line. Every reusable answer in my corpus is built on it. It kept my answers from becoming either nostalgia about past roles or AI cosplay, and the stop line alone probably saved me from talking my way out of good moments. Rambling past the answer costs more than a short answer ever does.

By the end I noticed the corpus had quietly become the real product of the search. Every process taught something reusable: which stories had pull, which claims needed proof, which gaps were real, which language sounded like me versus a performance. The offer was the outcome, but the corpus was what I'd actually built.

## What didn't work

Warm signal over-predicted outcomes, badly. Twice I had an engaged champion, strong calls, real momentum, and a fast rejection anyway. Warmth is not an offer. What matters is whether the role actually exists as described, whether the hiring team agrees on its shape, and whether you map to their current constraint better than the competing profile does. I lost at least two processes to candidates who simply fit the movie better, and no amount of interview performance was going to change that.

Prep sometimes over-predicted personality. I'd walk in braced for a cold interviewer who turned out warm, or the reverse. The fix, which is now baked into /prep: predict structure, not personality. Likely evaluation criteria, likely anxieties, likely proof points. Whether someone will be friendly is not knowable and not useful.

Some gaps were real. A few roles wanted depth I genuinely didn't have, and the right move was never to talk around it. Name the gap, then bridge to the nearest real evidence. Denying a gap the interviewer can already see costs you the thing you actually have, which is credibility.

## The outcome, with caveats

The company I signed with was the first process where the role shape, the team's expectations, and my actual spike all pointed the same direction. Role shape mattered more than title: the right mandate under a modest title beats the wrong one under an impressive one. I signed in July 2026 and start on July 20.

The caveats, plainly. The system did not get me the offer; it made me a better-calibrated candidate across eighteen chances, and one of them converged. Cloning this repo gives you the machinery, not the results. The tuning is the work: your debriefs have to be honest, your corpus entries have to be in your own voice, and you have to keep feeding it after the interviews that go badly, which is exactly when you won't want to. What I'd do differently: start /scout in week one instead of month two, and treat every warm process as unconverted until the offer call.

The search is a market-feedback loop. This repo is just the loop, written down.
